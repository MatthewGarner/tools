An initiative list hides its claims: everything on it reads as certain, nothing has a price for being wrong, and nothing ever dies. Write yours as explicit bets instead — stake, odds, payoff, and the line that kills it — and get a deck-ready board with the portfolio's real odds of losing money, plus the audits that shame the lines that can't say it straight.

    Why state it as bets?

    An initiative list is a place where claims go to hide. Every line reads as equally certain, nothing on it has a price for being wrong, and nothing on it is ever allowed to die — so the portfolio just grows until a re-org clears it. Restating each line as a bet forces the sentence a roadmap slide never makes you say out loud: "we're staking X at these odds for this payoff, and we fold when Y." A line that can't complete that sentence is the one worth questioning.

    Every stake, odds and payoff is a 90% range, run through a seeded Monte Carlo pass 4,000 times. That one pass answers two different questions on purpose: the per-bet band asks is this bet sound (expected value from the odds and payoff you gave it, independent of what else is in the portfolio), while the portfolio fan asks what might actually happen (each bet resolves win or lose, summed across the book) — which is why the same numbers can show a healthy median bet next to a portfolio with a real chance of losing money. The audits are the parts a slide usually buries: NO KILL CRITERION when nothing says when to fold, ODDS IMPLY CERTAINTY when a range is effectively a fact dressed up as a forecast, and LOSES AT P50 when the median outcome is already a loss.
