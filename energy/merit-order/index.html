What sets the price

Every generator offers power at its marginal cost — roughly its fuel cost: near zero for wind and solar, low for nuclear, higher for gas. Stack them cheapest-first and the demand line lands on one plant, the marginal unit. In a pay-as-clear market everyone dispatched is paid that one plant's price — so a wind-heavy day is still priced by gas whenever the last megawatt needed comes from a CCGT, and the cheap plants pocket the difference (their inframarginal rent).

Push renewables up and demand down and the marginal unit becomes a must-run plant bidding below zero — subsidised wind that would rather pay to keep generating than lose its per-MWh subsidy, or inflexible thermal that can't cheaply switch off. The clearing price goes negative: a signal to switch off, not a glitch.

This is a single snapshot of uniform-price, day-ahead-style clearing — deliberately not GB's real-time balancing mechanism, which runs pay-as-bid. Each plant offers a flat marginal cost (no rising heat-rate curves, unit-commitment or start-up costs), bids equal true costs (no strategic bidding), and must-run inflexibility is stood in for by a negative bid with no explicit curtailment — the "would generate anyway" megawatts are simplified away. Illustrative plants, no live data, no network constraints.

Sibling toys extend the same engine: network constraints turn one price into locational prices, an interconnector couples two markets, the marginal plant's carbon is the carbon of the next megawatt, and a price cap opens the capacity-market "missing money" gap. Here it's one price, one place.
