Cycle budget

A battery's warranty is a budget and every cycle spends it. One set of beliefs — spreads, fade, calendar ageing — priced at three horizons: what a cycle is worth today, whether the second cycle pays this year, and when augmentation stops being early and starts being late.

Try:
    Syntax reference
    battery: 100MW / 200MWhnameplate power / energy (required)
    spread: 35..85£/MWh: the day's best achieved spread, 90% range across days (required)
    charge: 15..45£/MWh buy price for the efficiency penalty; assumed 45% of spread if omitted
    second: 35..60%second cycle's spread as % of the day's best — a good second happens on a good day
    drift: -4..0 %/yrannual spread trend; flat (and flattering) if omitted
    rte: 86..90%round-trip efficiency (required)
    fade: 0.006..0.012 %/cyclecycle ageing, % of nameplate per full cycle (required)
    calendar: 1.0..1.8 %/yrcalendar ageing — happens anyway (required)
    cycles: 6000 over 15yrwarranty budget; over also sets the horizon (required)
    augment: 120..180 £/kWhenables the augment-when band
    discount: 7..10%default 8%

    Spread/second describe day-to-day variation; every other range is your uncertainty about a parameter. The engine treats them differently.

    What a cycle is worth

    Desks argue "should we cycle for this spread?" with instinct; warranty managers answer with a cap; this tool answers with a price. A cycle destroys a little capacity forever — that has a value — and it spends one unit of a finite warranty budget — that has a shadow price whenever the budget rations you below the days worth cycling. The dispatch threshold is whichever is higher, and the tool says which one is doing the work: "cycles are worth £41 each; it's the warranty doing the rationing, not the wear." The second cycle and the augmentation window are the same beliefs read at the year and asset-life horizons — a policy difference and a re-simulated remainder, never a partial formula.

    Named simplifications, so the tool can't oversell itself: spread is the achieved spread — market depth and dispatch quality are your problem, not the model's. Degradation is linear in cycles and time on the ranges you state — a belief container, not electrochemistry; bring your OEM's curve as a range, not a point. The charge-price leg is a level, not a shape. Augmentation restores nameplate and inherits the remaining warranty budget. And spreads stay flat over the life unless you say otherwise with drift: — omitting it flatters augmentation, and the tool warns you so.

    The tool prices your stated beliefs; it never claims the market will look like your ranges, and it isn't a dispatch optimiser. Sources: Douglas Hubbard on calibrated 90% intervals (How to Measure Anything).
